## Renting a Scooter in Bali

A scooter is the most popular way to explore Bali independently. With a scooter, you can weave through traffic, discover hidden beaches, stop at roadside warungs, and explore at your own pace. Around 70% of long-term visitors and a significant portion of short-term tourists opt for scooter rental. But it is not without risks — this guide covers everything you need to know to ride safely and legally.

## Costs

### Rental Rates
- **Daily:** IDR 70,000-150,000 (USD 5-10)
- **Weekly:** IDR 350,000-700,000 (USD 23-47)
- **Monthly:** IDR 800,000-1,500,000 (USD 53-100)
- **Newer/premium bikes (Nmax, PCX):** IDR 120,000-200,000 per day

### Fuel
Petrol is very cheap in Bali:
- **At Pertamina stations:** IDR 10,000-12,000 per liter (government-regulated price)
- **Roadside bottle vendors:** IDR 12,000-15,000 per liter (convenience markup)
- A full tank (5-6 liters) costs approximately IDR 60,000-70,000 and lasts 100-150km

### Other Costs
- **Parking:** IDR 2,000-5,000 per stop at most attractions and shops
- **Helmet:** Should be included with rental (always confirm)
- **Phone mount:** IDR 30,000-50,000 to buy — very useful for navigation

## Legal Requirements

### International Driving Permit (IDP)
Technically, you need an **International Driving Permit with motorcycle endorsement** to legally ride a scooter in Bali. This must be obtained in your home country before travel.

The reality: enforcement is inconsistent. Many tourists ride without an IDP and never encounter issues. However:
- **Police checkpoints** do occasionally stop tourists, especially in Kuta, Seminyak, and Canggu
- **Fine for no license:** IDR 500,000-1,000,000 (USD 33-67) — negotiable on the spot, which is effectively a bribe
- **Insurance claims** may be denied if you have an accident without a valid IDP
- **Hospital bills** — your travel insurance almost certainly will not cover scooter injuries without a valid license

**Our recommendation:** Get an IDP before you travel. It costs USD 15-20 in most countries, takes minutes to obtain, and protects you legally and financially.

## Choosing a Rental

### Where to Rent
- **Your accommodation** — many hotels and guesthouses rent scooters or can arrange one. Most convenient option.
- **Rental shops** — found on nearly every major street in tourist areas
- **Apps** — Klook and local apps offer scooter delivery services

### What to Check Before Riding
- **Brakes** — test both front and rear brakes before leaving
- **Lights** — headlight, tail light, and turn signals all working
- **Tires** — adequate tread depth, no visible damage
- **Mirrors** — both present and adjustable
- **Horn** — essential for Bali traffic communication
- **Fuel level** — start with at least a quarter tank
- **Helmet** — must be included. A proper helmet (not just a thin plastic one)

### Document the Condition
Take photos of any existing scratches or damage before riding away. This protects you from being charged for pre-existing damage when you return the bike.

## Safety Tips

### Essential Safety Practices
- **Always wear a helmet** — head injuries are the leading cause of serious scooter accidents in Bali
- **Wear closed-toe shoes** — flip-flops provide zero protection in an accident
- **Long pants are advisable** — road rash is painful
- **Drive defensively** — assume other drivers will do unexpected things
- **Use turn signals** and check mirrors before changing lanes
- **Keep your speed moderate** — most tourist accidents involve excessive speed on unfamiliar roads
- **Never drink and ride** — Bali's roads are challenging enough sober

### Bali-Specific Hazards
- **Potholes and uneven surfaces** — especially on rural roads
- **Dogs on the road** — Bali's street dogs frequently cross without warning
- **Offerings on the road** — canang sari on the road surface can be slippery
- **Monkeys** — particularly near Ubud and temple areas
- **Sudden rain** — wet roads become very slippery very quickly
- **Other scooters** — carrying impossible loads, riding the wrong way, no helmets
- **Large trucks and buses** — they do not give way to scooters

### If You Have an Accident
1. Move to safety if possible
2. Call emergency services (118 for ambulance, 112 for general emergency)
3. Contact your travel insurance provider
4. Document everything with photos
5. Get a police report if injuries or significant damage occurred
6. Contact your rental provider

## Best Scooter Routes

- **Canggu to Tanah Lot** — Scenic coastal ride through rice paddies (30 minutes)
- **Ubud to Tegallalang** — Through rice terraces and small villages (20 minutes)
- **Uluwatu Peninsula loop** — Dramatic clifftop roads between beaches (1-2 hours)
- **East Bali coast** — Candidasa to Amed along the scenic coastal road (1.5 hours)
- **Munduk and the highlands** — Cool mountain roads through coffee and clove plantations (challenging but rewarding)

## Alternatives to Scooters

If you are not comfortable riding a scooter, you still have excellent options:
- **Grab/Gojek motorbike taxi** — let someone else ride while you sit on the back
- **Private driver** — IDR 500,000-700,000 for a full day with AC car
- **Bicycle rental** — IDR 30,000-50,000 per day for short-range exploring in flat areas
- **Walking** — Central Ubud and beachfront areas like Sanur are walkable

Scooters open up Bali in a way no other transport can. But they also come with real risks. If you are an experienced rider, it is one of the best ways to explore the island. If you are a beginner, invest time in a quiet area (like the back roads of Sanur) to build confidence before tackling busy routes.
