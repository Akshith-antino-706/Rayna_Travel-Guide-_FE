## How Safe Is Bali?

Bali is generally a safe destination for tourists. Violent crime against visitors is rare, the local population is welcoming, and millions of tourists visit each year without incident. However, like any popular tourist destination, Bali has its risks — primarily petty theft, traffic accidents, scams, and health concerns. Understanding these risks allows you to enjoy Bali confidently while taking sensible precautions.

## Petty Crime and Theft

The most common safety issue for tourists in Bali is petty theft:

- **Bag snatching** from motorbikes — thieves on scooters grab bags from pedestrians or other riders. Carry bags on the side away from the road, or use a cross-body bag.
- **Beach theft** — Never leave valuables unattended on the beach. Use a waterproof pouch for essentials.
- **Pickpocketing** in crowded markets (Ubud Market, Kuta) — keep valuables in front pockets or a money belt.
- **Hotel room theft** — Use room safes for passports, cash, and electronics. Lock your door.

**How to protect yourself:**
- Use hotel room safes
- Do not flash expensive jewelry, watches, or large amounts of cash
- Carry only what you need for the day
- Keep phone secure (not in back pocket on a scooter)
- Use a money belt for carrying larger amounts

## Scams to Watch For

### Money Exchange Scams
Some unlicensed money changers use counting tricks, hidden fees, or distraction techniques to short-change you. Only use licensed exchangers (BMC, Central Kuta) with posted rates and receipts.

### Taxi Scams
Unlicensed taxi drivers at the airport and tourist areas may overcharge significantly. Use Grab/Gojek or official airport taxis with fixed prices.

### Fake Tour Operators
Some street touts sell tours at inflated prices with poor service. Book through your hotel, reputable agencies, or established platforms (Klook, GetYourGuide).

### ATM Skimming
Card skimming devices on standalone ATMs are a known issue. Use ATMs inside banks or shopping malls, cover the keypad when entering your PIN, and monitor your bank statements.

### Overcharging at Markets
Not technically a scam — bargaining is expected at traditional markets. But uninformed tourists can pay 3-5x fair prices. Research typical prices beforehand and bargain respectfully.

## Traffic Safety

**This is the single biggest safety risk in Bali.** Scooter accidents involving tourists are extremely common. Key risks:

- Chaotic traffic with unpredictable driving behavior
- Poor road conditions (potholes, uneven surfaces)
- No lane discipline
- Animals on the road
- Drunk driving (especially at night near nightlife areas)

**Protect yourself:**
- Always wear a helmet on scooters (proper helmets, not thin plastic ones)
- Do not ride a scooter if you are inexperienced
- Never drink and drive/ride
- Wear closed-toe shoes and long pants when riding
- Use a private driver for unfamiliar routes
- Cross streets carefully — pedestrians do not have right of way

## Health Risks

### Bali Belly
Stomach upsets are common for first-time visitors. Minimize risk by:
- Drinking only bottled or filtered water
- Eating at busy warungs (high turnover = fresh food)
- Avoiding raw salads at questionable establishments
- Washing hands frequently
- Starting with cooked foods and gradually introducing street food

### Mosquito-Borne Diseases
Dengue fever is present in Bali, particularly during wet season. Use DEET-based repellent, wear long sleeves at dawn and dusk, and stay in accommodation with screens or AC.

### Rabies
Bali has a rabies problem, primarily from stray dogs. Avoid touching or approaching stray animals. If bitten, seek immediate medical attention.

### Sun and Heat
Tropical sun causes burns quickly. Use SPF 50+ sunscreen, stay hydrated, and avoid prolonged midday sun exposure.

## Natural Hazards

### Volcanic Activity
Mount Agung is an active volcano that last erupted in 2017-2019. Monitor official alerts (BNPB — Indonesia's disaster agency) and follow evacuation instructions if warnings are issued. Most tourist areas are outside danger zones.

### Ocean Conditions
Strong rip currents, powerful waves, and hidden reef are present at many Bali beaches. Swim only at patrolled beaches, respect warning flags, and never swim alone in unfamiliar waters.

### Earthquakes
Indonesia lies in the Pacific Ring of Fire. Minor earthquakes are fairly common. Know your hotel's evacuation procedures and move to open ground if shaking is strong.

## Solo Female Traveler Safety

Bali is considered one of the safest destinations in Southeast Asia for solo female travelers. Catcalling is uncommon, and Balinese culture is respectful. Sensible precautions include:
- Avoid walking alone in isolated areas at night
- Use Grab/Gojek rather than hailing random vehicles
- Share your itinerary with someone back home
- Trust your instincts
- Canggu and Ubud have strong solo female traveler communities

## Travel Insurance

Travel insurance is not optional for Bali — it is essential. Ensure your policy covers:
- Medical treatment and hospital stays
- Emergency medical evacuation (to Singapore or Australia if needed)
- Scooter/motorbike riding (many policies exclude this)
- Water sports and adventure activities
- Trip cancellation and delays

Bali is a safe destination where common sense goes a long way. The vast majority of safety issues are preventable with basic awareness and preparation. Do not let safety concerns deter you from visiting — just take the same precautions you would in any major tourist destination.
