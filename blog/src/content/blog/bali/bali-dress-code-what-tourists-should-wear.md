## What to Wear in Bali

Bali is a tropical island with a relaxed atmosphere, but it is also a deeply spiritual place with important cultural dress codes. Understanding what to wear — and when — shows respect for Balinese culture and ensures you are welcome everywhere. This guide covers dress expectations for every situation you will encounter.

## Temple Dress Code (Mandatory)

Bali's Hindu temples have strict dress requirements that are non-negotiable:

- **Sarong (kamen):** A wraparound cloth that covers your legs to below the knee. Both men and women must wear one.
- **Sash (selendang):** A cloth sash tied around the waist over the sarong.
- **Covered shoulders:** Sleeveless tops are not appropriate. Wear at least a t-shirt.
- **No revealing clothing:** Short shorts, bikini tops, and crop tops are not permitted.

Most major temples (Tanah Lot, Uluwatu, Tirta Empul, Besakih) provide sarongs and sashes for rent or loan at the entrance, typically for free or IDR 10,000-20,000. However, buying your own sarong at a market (IDR 30,000-80,000) is more convenient and hygienic, and you will use it repeatedly throughout your trip.

**Important cultural note:** Women who are menstruating are traditionally asked not to enter Balinese temple grounds. This is a deeply held spiritual belief, and while enforcement varies, it is respectful to be aware of this custom.

## Beach and Pool Wear

At beaches, pools, and beach clubs, typical resort wear is perfectly acceptable:
- Swimsuits, bikinis, and board shorts
- Cover-ups and sarongs for walking to and from the beach
- Flip-flops or sandals

**Do not wear swimwear** when walking through towns, visiting shops, or eating at restaurants away from the beach. This is considered disrespectful, even in tourist areas.

## Restaurant Dress Code

- **Warungs and casual restaurants:** Casual clothing — shorts, t-shirts, sundresses, sandals. No specific dress code.
- **Mid-range restaurants:** Smart casual. Clean shorts or pants, a neat top, and sandals or closed shoes.
- **Fine dining:** Smart casual to semi-formal. Long pants or a nice dress, closed-toe shoes preferred. Some upscale venues may turn away guests in flip-flops or beachwear.
- **Beach clubs:** Stylish resort wear. Most beach clubs have a smart-casual dress code — no sports jerseys or dirty clothing.

## Nightlife and Clubs

Bali's nightclubs and bars generally require:
- Smart casual at minimum
- No flip-flops at upscale venues (some are more relaxed)
- Collared shirts preferred at some establishments
- No singlets or sleeveless tops for men at certain clubs

## Everyday Exploring

For general sightseeing, markets, and getting around:
- Light, breathable clothing (cotton or moisture-wicking fabrics)
- Shorts and t-shirts are fine in tourist areas
- Cover shoulders and knees when visiting villages away from tourist zones
- Comfortable walking shoes or sturdy sandals
- A light rain jacket or poncho (especially November-March)

## Packing Suggestions for Bali

### Essentials
- 2-3 light, breathable outfits
- 1-2 swimsuits
- A sarong (or buy one in Bali)
- Comfortable walking shoes that slip on and off easily (you remove shoes at temples)
- Flip-flops or sandals
- A light cardigan or long-sleeve top (for temples and air-conditioned spaces)
- Sun hat or cap
- Sunglasses

### Nice to Have
- Light rain jacket (wet season)
- Reef-safe sunscreen
- Mosquito repellent
- Small daypack for excursions
- Reusable water bottle

### What to Skip
- Heavy clothing — you will not need it (even Ubud, at 600m elevation, rarely drops below 22°C)
- Formal wear — unless you have a specific fine-dining reservation
- Too many outfits — laundry services are cheap in Bali (IDR 10,000-15,000 per kg)

## Fabric and Climate Considerations

Bali's climate is hot and humid year-round (average 27-30°C with 70-80% humidity). Choose:
- **Cotton and linen** for breathability
- **Quick-dry fabrics** for active days
- **Light colors** that reflect heat
- **Avoid heavy denim** — it is too hot and takes forever to dry

Lightweight, respectful clothing that can transition from temple to restaurant to beach is the key to smart Bali packing. A sarong is the single most versatile item you can carry — it serves as temple wear, beach cover-up, picnic blanket, and even a light blanket on chilly air-conditioned buses.
