## What to Wear in Delhi

Delhi is not as strict about dress codes as some travelers expect, but dressing appropriately for the climate, culture, and specific situations will make your trip more comfortable and respectful. India's capital is cosmopolitan — you will see everything from traditional saris to Western business suits on the same street — but a few guidelines will help you navigate different settings.

## General Guidelines

Delhi does not have a mandatory dress code for public spaces, and you will not face legal consequences for wearing Western clothing. However, dressing modestly is appreciated and will reduce unwanted attention, especially for women.

### For Women
- **Recommended:** Loose-fitting trousers or long skirts, tops that cover shoulders, light scarves
- **Works well:** Linen pants, cotton kurtas (Indian tunics — comfortable, cheap, and culturally appropriate), maxi dresses, palazzo pants
- **Avoid in public:** Very short shorts, low-cut tops, transparent clothing, and tight-fitting activewear
- **Note:** In upscale South Delhi neighborhoods and malls, Western casual clothing including jeans, T-shirts, and summer dresses is completely normal

### For Men
- **Recommended:** Long trousers or shorts below the knee, collared shirts or T-shirts, closed-toe shoes for monument visits
- **Works well:** Cotton chinos, linen shirts, polo shirts, lightweight kurta-pajama
- **Avoid:** Going shirtless in public (even in extreme heat), very short shorts
- **Note:** Men face fewer dress-related judgments, but dressing neatly improves interactions everywhere

## Dressing for Religious Sites

Delhi is home to temples, mosques, gurudwaras, churches, and shrines of virtually every religion. Each has slightly different expectations.

### Temples (Hindu, Jain)
- Remove shoes before entering
- Cover shoulders and knees
- Some temples prohibit leather items (belts, wallets) inside
- **Examples:** Akshardham Temple, Birla Mandir, Lotus Temple

### Mosques
- Remove shoes
- Women must cover heads, arms, and legs (scarves usually available at entrance)
- Men should wear long trousers
- **Examples:** Jama Masjid (provides coverings), Nizamuddin Dargah

### Gurudwaras (Sikh Temples)
- Remove shoes (shoe storage available at entrance)
- Cover your head (scarves/bandanas available free)
- No alcohol or tobacco should be consumed before visiting
- **Examples:** Gurudwara Bangla Sahib, Gurudwara Sis Ganj Sahib

### Churches
- Modest clothing — shoulders and knees covered
- No head covering required
- **Examples:** Sacred Heart Cathedral, St. James' Church

## Seasonal Clothing Guide

Delhi has extreme seasonal variation — from near-freezing winters to scorching summers — so pack according to your travel month.

### Winter (November–February)
Delhi winters are colder than most visitors expect. December and January mornings can drop to 3-5°C with dense fog.
- **Daytime:** Layers — a light sweater or jacket over a T-shirt
- **Evening:** Warm jacket, scarf, and closed-toe shoes essential
- **Pack:** Thermal undergarments for January visits, a warm coat, wool socks
- **Tip:** Delhi's fog makes mornings particularly cold and damp

### Spring (March–April)
Pleasant to warm weather, perfect for sightseeing.
- **Daytime:** Light cotton or linen clothing
- **Evening:** A light layer for cooler evenings
- **Pack:** Sunscreen, sunglasses, comfortable walking shoes

### Summer (May–June)
Extreme heat — temperatures exceed 40°C regularly, touching 48°C in some years.
- **Daytime:** Lightest possible cotton or linen, loose-fitting, light-colored
- **Evening:** Still hot — lightweight clothing continues
- **Pack:** Wide-brimmed hat, strong sunscreen (SPF 50+), electrolyte packets
- **Tip:** Carry a small towel for wiping sweat

### Monsoon (July–September)
Hot, humid, and rainy. Prepare for sudden downpours.
- **Daytime:** Quick-dry fabrics, waterproof shoes or sturdy sandals
- **Evening:** Light layers for air-conditioned spaces
- **Pack:** Compact umbrella, waterproof bag for electronics, extra pair of shoes
- **Tip:** Avoid suede and leather shoes — they will not survive monsoon puddles

## Dressing for Specific Situations

### Fine Dining
Delhi's upscale restaurants generally expect smart casual — collared shirts, closed-toe shoes, and no flip-flops. Some luxury hotel restaurants like Bukhara or Indian Accent have stricter dress codes.

### Nightclubs and Bars
Most Delhi clubs enforce a dress code: no flip-flops, no shorts, no sleeveless shirts for men. Women have more flexibility. Carry a valid photo ID (passport or copy) as clubs check age.

### Shopping in Old Delhi
Wear comfortable, modest clothing that you do not mind getting a bit dusty. The narrow lanes can be hot and crowded. Closed-toe shoes protect your feet.

### Business Meetings
Indian business culture expects formal attire — suits or smart business casual for both men and women.

## Practical Packing Tips

- **Buy local:** Cotton kurtas, dupattas (scarves), and kolhapuri sandals are inexpensive, comfortable, and culturally appropriate. Markets like Janpath and Sarojini Nagar sell these for ₹200-500
- **Carry a large scarf or shawl** — useful for temple visits, sun protection, air-conditioned restaurants, and as a blanket on trains
- **Pack neutral colors** — they show dust and stains less than white
- **Comfortable shoes are essential** — you will walk 10,000-15,000 steps daily on uneven surfaces
- **Laundry is cheap** — most hotels offer laundry service, and dhobi (laundry) services charge ₹50-100 per kg. Pack light and wash frequently
