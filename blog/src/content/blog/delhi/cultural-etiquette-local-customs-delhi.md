## Understanding Delhi's Culture

Delhi is a city of extraordinary cultural depth and diversity. As the capital of a country with over 1.4 billion people speaking hundreds of languages and practicing dozens of religions, Delhi is a microcosm of India itself. Understanding local customs and etiquette will not only help you avoid awkward situations but will also open doors to deeper, more meaningful interactions.

## Greetings and Social Interaction

### The Namaste
The traditional Indian greeting is **Namaste** (or Namaskar) — pressing your palms together at chest level with a slight bow. This is universally understood and appreciated, and is appropriate in virtually every situation. Most Delhiites also shake hands, especially in business or cosmopolitan settings.

### Addressing People
- Use **Sir** or **Ma'am** for people older than you — this is common and shows respect
- **Bhaiya** (brother) and **Didi** (sister) are friendly terms used for shopkeepers, rickshaw drivers, and others
- First names are fine in casual settings, but add **ji** as a suffix for respect (e.g., Rahul-ji)

### Physical Contact
- Handshakes between men are common
- Many traditional Indian women may prefer a namaste to a handshake with male strangers
- Public displays of affection between couples are frowned upon — hand-holding is fine in modern areas, but kissing or embracing in public attracts negative attention
- Same-sex physical affection between male friends (hand-holding, arm around shoulder) is a common sign of friendship, not romantic interest

## Dining Etiquette

### Eating with Your Hands
Traditional Indian food — roti, naan, dosa, rice — is often eaten with the hands. Use your **right hand only** for eating, as the left hand is considered unclean. If you are not comfortable eating with your hands, cutlery is always available at restaurants.

### Vegetarianism
India has the world's largest vegetarian population. Many restaurants are exclusively vegetarian. When invited to someone's home, ask about dietary preferences before bringing food gifts. In Delhi, vegetarian food is not a limitation — it is a celebrated cuisine with extraordinary variety.

### Guest Etiquette
If invited to an Indian home:
- **Bring a gift** — sweets (mithai), fruit, or flowers are appropriate. Avoid leather items for Hindu and Jain households.
- **Remove shoes** at the entrance
- **Accept food and drink offerings** — refusing hospitality can be perceived as rude. At minimum, accept a glass of water or cup of chai.
- **Expect generous portions** — your host will insist you eat more. A polite decline after a reasonable amount is fine.

### Alcohol
Delhi is relatively liberal about alcohol compared to many Indian cities. It is available at restaurants, bars, and retail shops. However, avoid consuming alcohol in public spaces, near religious sites, or during religious festivals. Two dry days per year (Republic Day and Independence Day) prohibit alcohol sales.

## Religious Customs

### Temple Visits
- Remove shoes before entering
- Walk clockwise around the main shrine
- Accept prasad (blessed food) with your right hand
- Small donations are appreciated (₹11, ₹21, ₹51 — odd numbers are considered auspicious)
- Do not touch idols or sacred objects without permission
- Photography rules vary — ask before taking photos inside sanctums

### Mosque Visits
- Remove shoes at the entrance
- Women should cover their hair
- Avoid visiting during prayer times (especially Friday afternoon)
- Do not walk in front of people who are praying
- Photography of worshippers requires permission

### Gurudwara Visits (Sikh Temples)
- Cover your head (free scarves provided)
- Remove shoes (free shoe storage available)
- Do not bring tobacco or alcohol
- The langar (community kitchen) serves free vegetarian food to all visitors regardless of religion — participating is encouraged and a beautiful experience

## Social Norms

### Photography
- Always ask permission before photographing people, especially women and children
- Photography is prohibited in some sections of monuments, museums, and all military installations
- Avoid photographing beggars or people in distress — it is exploitative

### Bargaining
Bargaining is expected and even enjoyed in street markets, bazaars, and with auto-rickshaw drivers. It is **not** appropriate at fixed-price shops, malls, government emporiums, or restaurants. A good rule: start at 40-50% of the first asking price in markets and settle around 60-70%.

### Personal Space
Delhi is crowded, and personal space norms are different from Western countries. People may stand closer to you in queues, on public transport, and in markets. This is normal, not aggressive.

### Head Wobble
The famous Indian head wobble — a side-to-side movement — generally means "yes," "I understand," or "I agree." It is not a "no." This confuses many first-time visitors but becomes intuitive quickly.

### Left Hand
The left hand is traditionally considered unclean. Use your right hand for:
- Giving and receiving money
- Handing items to people
- Eating
- Handshakes

## Common Courtesies

- **Remove shoes** when entering homes and religious spaces
- **Avoid pointing feet** at people or religious objects — feet are considered the lowest part of the body
- **Do not point at people** with your finger — use an open hand or chin nod instead
- **Respect elders** — offering your seat on public transport to elderly people is expected
- **Avoid discussing** Pakistan, Kashmir, or caste politics unless you understand the nuances deeply
- **Sacred cows** roam Delhi's streets freely — never disturb or disrespect them

## The Concept of "Jugaad"

Delhi runs on **jugaad** — a Hindi term meaning creative improvisation or finding a workaround. If something does not work as planned (and in India, this happens), embrace flexibility. Plans change, buses are late, and shop owners may be on an unannounced break. Patience and a sense of humor are your most valuable travel tools in Delhi.

## Language Tips

While Hindi is Delhi's primary language, English is widely spoken in tourist areas, hotels, restaurants, and by younger people. Learning a few Hindi phrases earns enormous goodwill:

- **Namaste** — Hello
- **Dhanyavaad** — Thank you
- **Kitna?** — How much?
- **Bahut accha** — Very good
- **Nahi chahiye** — I don't need it (useful for persistent vendors)
- **Bhaiya, meter se chalo** — Brother, go by the meter (for auto-rickshaws)
